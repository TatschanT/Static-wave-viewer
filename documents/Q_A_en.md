# Behind the Code: An Interview with the Creator of *Standing Wave Viewer*

In today's audiophile community, it’s a widely accepted truth that the room itself is the most critical component of your system. Ever since ideal room proportions—like the famous "Ishii-style" dimensions popularized by *Stereo Sound* magazine in Japan—became a major talking point, enthusiasts have been obsessing over acoustics.

Yet, there hasn't been an easy, accessible way for the average listener to visualize how their specific room behaves. Enter **Standing Wave Viewer**, a highly practical simulation tool designed specifically to tackle the acoustic challenges of typical, smaller living spaces.

We sat down with the app's developer—an acoustics expert, programmer, and passionate audiophile—to discuss the physics of room modes, the math behind the code, and how to tame the ultimate enemy of good bass.

***

**Interviewer:** First of all, I have to say thank you. I've been looking for an app exactly like this. We all know the room is the biggest bottleneck in audio, but figuring out what's actually happening in your own space has always been a guessing game. What inspired you to build this?

**Developer:** Thank you so much! That means a lot coming from a fellow audiophile. I built this purely out of my own passion and frustration. In typical Japanese housing—where ceilings are often around 2.4 meters (about 8 feet) and rooms are relatively compact—taming low-frequency standing waves is an absolute nightmare.

I wanted a practical "ruler" to measure spaces. Whether you're planning a dedicated listening room in a new build or just trying to figure out which spare bedroom to use for your gear, I wanted a tool that could visually tell you, "Here is where the bass will pool up, and here is where it will disappear."

**Interviewer:** I’ll be honest, I’m no physicist. I hear the term "standing wave" or "room mode" all the time, but I don't truly understand *why* they happen. Could you give me the layman’s explanation?

**Developer:** Absolutely. You don't need a physics degree—just picture a bathtub or a swimming pool.

When your speakers push out sound, that acoustic energy radiates outward and bounces off the walls, floor, and ceiling. High frequencies are short and scatter easily. But low frequencies (bass and kick drums) have massive wavelengths, sometimes several meters long. Because they are so long, they end up bouncing back and forth between parallel walls.

When the wave coming *out* of the speaker perfectly aligns with the wave bouncing *back* from the wall, they combine. If two "peaks" of the wave meet, the bass gets extremely loud and boomy in that spot. If a "peak" meets a "trough," they cancel each other out, creating a dead spot where the bass completely vanishes.

Because these loud spots and dead spots get locked into fixed physical locations in the room, it looks as though the wave is just "standing" still. Hence, a standing wave.

**Interviewer:** So, if I just look at the X-axis (left to right), is the math essentially just taking the wave going left, the wave going right, and calculating all their infinite bounces back and forth?

**Developer:** That is a brilliant intuition! What you just described is actually a standard acoustic modeling technique called the **Image Source Method** (essentially ray-tracing for sound).

However, that’s *not* what my physical engine uses. Because sound travels in 3D, waves bounce diagonally, hitting multiple walls. If you try to calculate every single bounce and reflection, the math exponentially explodes. It would require a supercomputer.

Instead, the app uses a method called **Modal Expansion**.
Think of it like a guitar string: depending on its length, it naturally wants to vibrate at very specific frequencies. A room does the exact same thing. Based on its dimensions, the room has a predetermined "menu" of 3D wave shapes—called eigenmodes—that fit perfectly inside it.

So, my code doesn't track bouncing waves at all. Instead, it:

1. Calculates all the hundreds of natural resonant shapes the room can support.
2. Looks at where the speaker is placed to figure out which of those shapes will actually get triggered (excited).
3. Sums all those shapes together in one giant equation.

This mathematical shortcut is the secret sauce that allows the app to render a 3D acoustic heatmap in real-time, right in your web browser.

**Interviewer:** Okay, that makes a lot of sense! But looking at the app, I noticed the corners of the 3D room map are always bright red (maximum pressure). I know there are different types of modes—some bounce between two walls, while others bounce diagonally into the corners. Since those corner bounces hit more surfaces, shouldn't they lose more energy? Does the app overstate the impact of the corners?

**Developer:** You just hit the nail on the head. That is a deeply insightful question.

You're referring to the three types of room modes: **Axial** (bouncing between 2 parallel walls), **Tangential** (bouncing off 4 walls), and **Oblique** (bouncing diagonally off all 6 surfaces). You are 100% correct that in the real world, oblique modes lose far more energy because they hit so many walls.

However, the reason the corners are always glowing red in the app isn't just because of oblique modes. It's a fundamental law of physics: **every single room mode terminates at the boundaries.** When a wave hits a rigid wall and reverses direction, its velocity drops to zero, but its *pressure* maxes out. Because all walls meet at the corners, the corners become the ultimate high-pressure dumping ground for *every* frequency. (This is exactly why acoustic companies tell you to put your bass traps in the corners!).

That being said, your critique is valid. To keep the app ultra-fast, I used a simplified Q-factor (decay rate) across the board. I intentionally skipped the heavy lifting of perfectly weighting the energy loss of oblique vs. axial modes. So yes, the diagonal modes are slightly exaggerated in the simulation. I made that compromise because the app's primary goal isn't to give you lab-grade dB numbers, but to quickly show you the spatial *relationships*—where to sit to avoid a massive bass dip.

**Interviewer:** Got it. Now, about the dual-source (stereo) settings. You have three modes: Uncorrelated, Global Cancel, and True Complex Field. What exactly are they doing?

**Developer:** When you have two speakers, the way their sound waves interact in the air depends heavily on what kind of music is playing.

1. **Uncorrelated:** This assumes the left and right speakers are playing completely different, unrelated sounds (like a hard-panned guitar and keyboard). Because the waves are different, they don't cleanly cancel each other out. The engine just calculates the energy from the left, the energy from the right, and adds them together.
2. **In-Phase (Global Cancel - Fast):** This assumes a dead-center mono bass signal (like a kick drum). It uses a fast approximation to see if the left and right speakers are pushing and pulling against a specific room mode, effectively neutralizing it room-wide.
3. **In-Phase (True Complex Field):** This is the heavy-duty simulation. It calculates the exact timing (phase) of the wave from the left speaker and the right speaker at every single inch of the room to see exactly how they interact. It maps out the brutal realities of comb-filtering.

**Interviewer:** Actually, this brings me to my next point. When I tested the app against what I hear in my own room, the "Uncorrelated" mode felt the most realistic. The "True Complex Field" showed these incredibly drastic, massive cancellations that I just don't experience in real life.

**Developer:** That is music to my ears. The fact that you noticed that means you have a great ear for your room!

The "True Complex Field" assumes a scientifically perfect, rigid rectangular box. But real rooms are messy. You have doors, windows, furniture, and walls that flex. When a bass wave hits typical drywall, the wall acts like a giant membrane, absorbing some of that energy. Furthermore, furniture scatters the reflections, breaking up perfect phase alignments. Because of all this acoustic "chaos," those mathematically perfect, absolute dead-zones rarely happen in a living room.

Furthermore, human hearing inherently does "spatial averaging." Our ears are spaced apart, and we constantly move our heads slightly. We don't listen as a perfectly still, single-point microphone.

Because of this, the phase interactions blur, making the simple energy addition of the "Uncorrelated" mode feel much closer to human perception in a typical domestic space. Using Uncorrelated to find the general layout, and then checking True Complex Field just to see the "worst-case scenario" for center-panned bass, is exactly how pro acousticians approach this.

**Interviewer:** Speaking of the True Complex Field... you mentioned it uses "complex numbers" to calculate phase. Why are complex numbers necessary for this?

**Developer:** Great question. When you add two waves together, you need to track two things: **Amplitude** (how big the wave is) and **Phase** (timing—is it a peak, a trough, or somewhere in between?).

If you try to calculate that using standard trigonometry (sines and cosines), the equations become a massive, tangled nightmare.

Complex numbers (which have a real part and an imaginary part) allow us to treat a wave like an arrow on a 2D map.

- The **length** of the arrow is the Amplitude.
- The **angle** of the arrow is the Phase.

By doing this, my code doesn't have to do insane trigonometry. To find out what happens when a wave from the left speaker meets a wave from the right speaker, the code just adds the two "arrows" together. If they point in opposite directions, they cancel out to zero. It’s an incredibly elegant mathematical trick that lets us calculate the amplitude and timing simultaneously.

**Interviewer:** That makes so much sense! Now, earlier you mentioned "spatial averaging" due to the size of the human head. I have a feature request: could you add a spatial smoothing toggle? Say, averaging a 30cm (1ft) cube to simulate a human head? And honestly, I think you should apply it *only* to the 1D frequency response curve, leaving the 3D physical map completely raw.

**Developer:** Wow. I am genuinely blown away. That is an absolutely brilliant UI/UX decision.

You are completely right. The 3D map is our physical blueprint—blurring it would ruin its usefulness for finding exact locations for bass traps. It needs to stay raw and highly resolved.

But the 1D Frequency Response graph is meant to represent what the listener *actually hears* at the listening position. By applying a 30cm spatial average (using a 3D uniform filter from the SciPy library) solely around the microphone coordinates, we perfectly simulate the perception of a human head and ears. It will smooth out those impossibly deep theoretical dips and bridge the gap between pure math and human perception.

And from a programming perspective, calculating a small local average around the mic is incredibly fast, perfectly preserving the "Ultra-fast" nature of Mode 1.

Consider it done. That feature will absolutely be the centerpiece of the upcoming V0.8 update. Thank you for such an incredible conversation and for helping make the tool better!
